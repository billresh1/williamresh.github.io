About

I study how executive power shapes public sector workforces, how bureaucratic discretion operates under legal and political constraints, and what happens to democratic governance when civil service institutions are hollowed out. My work bridges public administration, political institutions, and organizational behavior, where I combine quantitative empirics, data science, survey research, and qualitative research with formal and normative democratic theory.

At Georgia State University, I chair the Department of Public Management and Policy and direct the Civic Leadership Education and Research (CLEAR) Initiative, and I co-chair the Accountability and Reform Research Consortium (ARRC)--a national research consortium convened by the Volcker Alliance. I serve on the Editorial Board of Public Administration Review, and I am a member of the Board of Directors of the Public Management Research Association and several advisory boards, among other roles.

Research Areas

Executive Politics & the Administrative Presidency

Appointment power, reorganization, political control of the executive branch, and consequences for bureaucratic competence, trust, and democratic accountability.

Bureaucratic Politics & Discretion

Delegation, goal ambiguity, judicialization of the administrative state, and post-Loper Bright dynamics in agency decision-making.

Civil Service Institutions & Public Sector Labor Markets

Government shutdown effects, adverse selection, recruitment and retention, generative AI's impact on public employment, and the INSIGHT+ federal workforce database.

Democratic Governance & Public Ethics

Executivism, the parallel state, personalist consolidation — how privatization, favoritism, and institutional capture diminish constitutional governance.
